That has been one of the biggest headlines in Danish news this past week. As the US and Iran have gone to war, many Danes in the Middle East now find themselves trapped, unable to return to Denmark. It is indeed a failure that necessary precautions were not taken, given the months of unrest in Iran and tensions in the region.

> Maybe, one reason is that Danish authorities were not well-informed. According to [Globalnyt's](https://globalnyt.dk/udlandsdaekning2025/) 2025 report on foreign affairs coverage, about only 15% of Danish media's focus is on the Middle East, a figure dwarfed by a 52% share dedicated to coverage of Europe and trailing the 16% for North America.

Given this information gap, it can seem crazy to see US President Trump calling for [regime change in Iran](https://abcnews.com/Politics/trump-new-call-regime-change-iran-justified-imminent/story?id=130629024). To better understand how we got here, I would like to share an interview with an Iranian from my Danish class. My interviewee will use the name "Leila" for anonymity.

**Note: This interview was conducted days before the US attacks on Iran on 28 February 2026.**

**Elif:** How do you perceive the recent events unfolding in Iran?

**Leila:** The unrest of the last few months has especially been a reflection of public dissatisfaction towards the economic situation of Iran. To the country's poor governance which led to devaluation of the national currency -the Iranian Rial- and weaknesses across many sectors. As a result of the government's harsh response and repression, the protests gradually turned into calls for a change in government.

**Elif:** What were the primary goals of the protesters?

**Leila:** There is good evidence suggesting that the majority of protesters, back home in Iran, demand a change in the current system of governance and support the return of Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi as the shah of Iran. There is a strong public demand for the removal of what is widely perceived as a dictatorial regime.

> Reza Pahlavi is the former Crown Prince of Iran and the eldest son of the last Shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. He left Iran in 1978 at the age of 17 to attend pilot training in the United States. The following year, the Iranian Revolution led to the overthrow of his father and the abolition of the monarchy, and he has remained in exile ever since. He is a prominent figure in the opposition to the current Iranian government.

**Elif:** To what extent do these demands reflect the views of the general population?

**Leila:** The demands expressed by protesters we see in the street resonate with a large part of the population. Especially those affected by economic hardship and social restrictions.

**Elif:** What is the general public sentiment regarding potential U.S. involvement?

**Leila:** Protesters have faced severe and violent armed repression by the Iranian government. In this context, many view U.S. involvement as potentially helpful, since protesters are confronting military weapons while lacking means to defend their lives.

**Elif:** What kind of ultimate outcome or resolution is the public seeking?

**Leila:** This is not the first time demonistrations aimed at political change have been met with severe repression from the government. Protesters are demanding the ouster of the current regime and the return of Reza Pahlavi as Iran's leader.

**Elif:** What are the prevailing hopes for a peaceful or political resolution?

**Leila:** The protesters are solely seeking a complete change of government and are unwilling to negotiate with the current regime. Their demand is for a total replacement of the governing system with no compromise.

**Elif:** What is the experience or perspective of the diaspora (those living abroad) right now?

**Leila:** Iranians abroad have fully expressed their support by participating in demonstrations around the world in favor of regime change and the return of Reza Shah. During the complete internet blackout in Iran, Iranians of the diaspora raised their voices to the world and made the international community aware of the massacres carried out by the government. They also are currently sharing news about the arrest of doctors and nurses by the government, aiming to make the world aware of the crimes committed by the current regime and seeking international assistance.

> According to Statistics Denmark [(FOLK1C)](https://www.statbank.dk/20024 ), there were 28 738 people of Iranian origin living in Denmark (2025). They are concentrated in Copenhagen, Gladsaxe and Lyngby-Taarbæk as well as Aarhus and Odense.

Leila's final words underscore a vital point. In a world where narratives are fiercely contested, the voices of ordinary Iranians are often lost—suppressed by the state at home and overshadowed by external political maneuvering abroad.

With figures like Donald Trump and his propaganda machine dominating the discourse, the actual experiences of the Iranian people are frequently sidelined. Now, more than ever, there is a need for the media to cut through the noise and offer a platform for the people of Iran to speak for themselves. Their voices must be heard.
